It continued as a member of the merged organization known as Grounded Solutions Network starting in 2016, and Homestead\u2019s trust includes 260 permanently affordable homes.\u00a0<\/em><\/strong><\/p>\n\n\n\n<p class=\"\"><strong>Q1: Where is Homestead Community Land Trust located?<\/strong><br><strong>A1:<\/strong> Homestead is located in Seattle, Washington. Our service area spans King County, and through partnerships, we also support the expansion of community land trust (CLT) homeownership in neighboring Pierce County.<\/p>\n\n\n\n<p class=\"\"><strong>Q2: What is Homestead\u2019s mission?<\/strong><br><strong>A2:<\/strong> Homestead CLT creates stability, equity, and opportunity by developing land and housing in trust, giving lower-income households the opportunity to own a home that is affordable to them and remains affordable to future owners. Our work is expanding an equitable economy for housing to create vibrant communities, help repair the harms of housing discrimination, and protect vulnerable communities from displacement.<\/p>\n\n\n\n<p class=\"\"><strong>Q3: Why is being a member of Grounded Solutions Network important to Homestead?<\/strong><br><strong>A3:<\/strong> Grounded Solutions is a critical knowledge network and advocacy partner for advancing permanently affordable homeownership.<\/p>\n\n\n\n<p class=\"\"><strong>Q4: How many staff members does Homestead have?<\/strong><br><strong>A4:<\/strong> We currently have 15 staff members, with plans to grow as we scale up our impact.<\/p>\n\n\n\n<p class=\"\"><strong>Q5: What kinds of individuals\/groups does Homestead serve?<\/strong><br><strong>A5:<\/strong> We serve modest-income individuals and families, many of whom are first-generation homeowners. We also are the managing member of a Section 8 rental development serving very low-income households.<\/p>\n\n\n\n<p class=\"\"><strong>Q6: How has Homestead grown over the years?<\/strong><br><strong>A6:<\/strong> Homestead has grown from a grassroots effort into a professional, regionally respected organization. Since 2018, we\u2019ve doubled our staff, tripled our pipeline, and significantly expanded our partnerships. We\u2019ve also developed larger, more complex and ambitious projects, including net-zero energy homes.<\/p>\n\n\n\n<p class=\"\"><strong>Q7: What recent awards\/recognitions has Homestead received for its work?<\/strong><br><strong>A7:<\/strong> In 2025, King County honored Homestead with the Green Globe Award for Leadership in Green Built Environment for The Southard\u2014our net-zero energy, Salmon Safe, affordable homeownership development in Tukwila. We won a Built Green Hammer award in 2023.<\/p>\n\n\n\n<p class=\"\"><strong>Q8: What challenges has Homestead faced in recent years?<\/strong><br><strong>A8:<\/strong> Like many nonprofits, we\u2019ve navigated staffing transitions, rising construction costs, and changing political will. But our biggest challenge is systemic: the funding environment continues to favor rental housing, even as homeownership remains the primary pathway to generational wealth. We\u2019ve had to make a powerful case for equity in homeownership\u2014and we\u2019re not done yet.<\/p>\n\n\n\n<p class=\"\"><strong>Q9: What are some of Homestead\u2019s goals for 2025 and beyond?<\/strong><br><strong>A9:<\/strong> Our goals include having 1,000 homes in the trust by the end of 2032, expanding our technical assistance to other CLTs and strengthening our statewide advocacy efforts. We\u2019re also deepening our racial equity practices and exploring innovative models for stewardship and sustainability.<\/p>\n\n\n\n<p class=\"\"><strong>Q10: If you could collaborate with anyone, who would it be? Why?<\/strong><br><strong>A10:<\/strong> I\u2019d love to work with NextGen philanthropic advisors who are guiding young wealth-holders to align their capital with values of justice and regeneration. There\u2019s a powerful opportunity to mobilize significant investment in land-based justice\u2014fueling CLTs and other models that return land to collective stewardship. Together, we can help shift the economic model from one of extraction to one that is regenerative\u2014where wealth circulates in community, land is honored, and equity is rooted.<\/p>\n\n\n\n<p class=\"\"><strong>Q11: What do you\/did you love the most about your job?<\/strong><br><strong>A11:<\/strong> I love doing the work in and with community. I\u2019m deeply influenced by Liberation Theology, which teaches that my well-being is inextricably linked with the well-being of others. I love listening to the wisdom of the community and helping to fulfill the longing for home\u2014a longing for place, safety, connection, and healing. The greatest joy comes from knowing that what we create is a collective effort, grounded in shared liberation.<\/p>\n\n\n\n<p class=\"\"><strong>Q12: What experiences and past roles have led you to where you are today?<\/strong><br><strong>A12:<\/strong> I\u2019ve worn many hats\u2014strategist, theologian, change manager. I came to Homestead through a path that combined my experience in real estate development with a strong foundation in leading strategy and organizational change. But it\u2019s my spirituality and lived experience that ground me in a deep commitment to economic justice. I\u2019m driven to be a good ancestor: to make systems work better for those who\u2019ve been left behind\u2014and to reimagine those systems through a lens of love, equity, and liberation.<\/p>\n\n\n\n<p class=\"\"><strong>Q13: What\u2019s a project you\u2019re working on right now? Why is it meaningful to you?<\/strong><br><strong>A13:<\/strong> Homestead is partnering with Aya Community Land Trust\u2014a newly formed, Black-led organization focused on land-based justice\u2014in Tacoma\u2019s historically Black Hilltop neighborhood. Together, we\u2019re working on a transformational project on a full city block of land, creating permanently affordable homeownership rooted in community leadership, cultural identity, and self-determination. It\u2019s meaningful to me because it shows what justice can look like in place\u2014repairing harm, restoring access, and building community power. Supporting Aya\u2019s launch and walking alongside them in this work is both a privilege and a profound responsibility.<\/p>\n\n\n\n<p class=\"\"><strong>Q14: How do you define success?<\/strong><br><strong>A14:<\/strong> Success is when the people most impacted by injustice are not just invited in but also shape the agenda, set the terms, and own the ground we gather on. It\u2019s when land is no longer a tool of exclusion but a vessel for healing, belonging, and self-determination. Success is equity made tangible\u2014measured not in metrics alone, but in lives reclaimed, ancestors honored, and futures rewritten. It\u2019s when community wisdom leads, systems are reimagined, and love becomes the organizing principle. That\u2019s the kind of liberation I work for\u2014and the kind I believe is possible.<\/p>\n\n\n\n<p class=\"\"><strong>Q15: How can people stay up-to-date on news about Homestead?<\/strong><br><strong>A15:<\/strong> Follow us on social media (@homesteadclt on Instagram, Facebook, LinkedIn, and X) and subscribe to our newsletter at <a href=\"http:\/\/www.homesteadclt.org\">www.homesteadclt.org<\/a>.

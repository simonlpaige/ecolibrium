# Community AI Dividend Fund
> A design note for Commonweave Phase 1

---

## The Alaska Analogy

Alaska discovered it was sitting on oil. Rather than let oil companies pocket all of it, the state created a sovereign wealth fund from royalties. Every resident gets an annual check - not welfare, not charity - a dividend from a shared resource that belongs to everyone.

The mechanism: **identify the resource -> put extraction proceeds into a fund -> distribute to all residents -> watch poverty fall without destroying work incentive.**

That worked. 40+ years of evidence. No inflation spike. Employment unchanged. The critics were wrong.

---

## The AI Version of the Same Idea

Communities are about to sit on something with the same dynamic: AI productivity. A small business district where AI handles scheduling, invoicing, inventory, and customer service generates massive economic surplus. Right now, that surplus goes to the software vendor (Salesforce, ServiceTitan, whatever SaaS the business uses). That's a design choice, not a law of nature.

A Community AI Dividend Fund is the Alaska model, scaled to a neighborhood, municipality, or cooperative.

**The mechanism:**
1. Community acquires or builds shared AI tools (scheduling, legal, admin, logistics) rather than renting them from outside vendors
2. Surplus generated by those tools (labor time saved x prevailing wage, or direct revenue from services rendered) accrues to a community fund
3. Fund distributes a periodic dividend to all members - every household, not just business owners

This is not a federal program waiting on Congress. It's an HOA with a better investment thesis.

---

## What Makes It Work

**Resource must be genuinely shared.** The tools must be community-owned, not vendor-licensed. Open-source toolchains are essential. A co-op that licenses Salesforce doesn't have an asset - it has a subscription.

**Distribution must be unconditional.** The moment you attach conditions (you must work X hours, attend Y meetings, prove need), you've recreated bureaucracy and lost the disinflationary advantage. Dividends, not welfare.

**The fund must be ring-fenced.** Legally separate from operating budgets. Can't be raided for a new park. This is why the Alaska Permanent Fund has survived 12 governors - the legal structure made raiding it politically toxic.

**Governance must prevent capture.** One member, one vote on fund decisions. No proxy voting by large stakeholders. WaldoNet / NeighborhoodOS governance models apply directly.

---

## Preconditions (what Phase 1 must build first)

- Community-owned AI infrastructure (not just access - ownership)
- A legal entity capable of holding assets and distributing dividends (cooperative, trust, or municipal authority)
- A democratic governance layer for fund decisions (Decidim or equivalent)
- A directory of member organizations and individuals (Commonweave directory is already this)

None of these are invented here. Worker cooperatives (Mondragon), CLTs (community land trusts), and municipal wealth funds (Preston Model) have already solved the legal and governance pieces. Commonweave assembles the playbook.

---

## Sizing the Opportunity

An average US small business spends roughly $15,000-$25,000/year on SaaS subscriptions. A KC neighborhood commercial district with 50 businesses = $750K-$1.25M/year flowing out to external software vendors.

If a community cooperative builds or acquires equivalent open-source tooling, keeps that money local, and distributes 50% as a dividend:
- $375K-$625K/year into a community fund
- Divided across 500 member households = $750-$1,250/household/year

Not Alaska-scale. But enough to matter and a proof of concept the city would pay attention to.

---

## Connection to Commonweave Framework

This is not a new idea bolted on. It's the operational expression of:
- **Principle 1**: Universal Sufficiency (UBI as floor)
- **Principle 4**: Common Ownership of the Commons (AI output is a commons)
- **Phase 1**: Pre-Transfer infrastructure that must exist before any larger transition

The fund IS the transition mechanism at community scale - no federal action required to start, and a working example makes the federal argument easier to win.

## Next Steps (if we're building this)

1. Find a municipality with existing community land trust infrastructure (KC has the Westside CLT and others)
2. Map the local SaaS spend - what are neighborhood businesses actually paying per year
3. Identify one open-source replacement with the highest ROI (scheduling or invoicing is usually the win)
4. Draft cooperative charter and fund governance doc
5. Pilot with 5-10 businesses before declaring a model

# Steelman Alternatives

> Commonweave proposes a particular path: cooperative, commons-based, democratic, ecological infrastructure for a post-labor economy.
>
> It is not the only path. This file holds the strongest competing theories — stated as their proponents would state them, not as caricatures.
>
> If Commonweave is right, it has to earn its position against these. If Commonweave is wrong in part, the right answer is probably hidden in one of these.

Do not weaken alternatives. Make them strong enough to be uncomfortable.

---

## Format

Each alternative includes:

1. **The claim** — what this alternative argues, in its strongest form
2. **Best advocates** — who has made this case well
3. **Strongest evidence** — what backs it up
4. **What it gets right** — Commonweave should incorporate this
5. **Where it conflicts with Commonweave** — the actual disagreement
6. **What would make it the right answer** — conditions under which we should adopt it instead

---

## A1. Social-democratic reform

**The claim:** The state, properly democratized and adequately funded, can deliver universal sufficiency through public goods, redistribution, and regulated markets. Commons frameworks add complexity without significantly improving outcomes. The Nordic model already approximates the post-labor economy Commonweave imagines, just without the cooperative-ownership emphasis.

**Best advocates:** Tony Atkinson (*Inequality*), Anthony Giddens (*The Third Way*), Lane Kenworthy (*Social Democratic America*).

**Strongest evidence:** Nordic countries score highest on most well-being and equality measures. Universal healthcare, universal childcare, and high-quality public education are achieved through state delivery, not commons governance. Decommodification works without commons.

**What it gets right:**
- The state has unmatched capacity for redistribution at scale.
- Universal programs are politically resilient where means-tested ones aren't.
- Property law and democratic legitimacy are state functions; pretending otherwise is fragile.

**Where it conflicts with Commonweave:**
- Commonweave argues the state is captured at a structural level by capital and is not reliably reformable in the time available.
- Commonweave emphasizes ownership distribution, not just outcome distribution.
- Commonweave treats commons governance as a generative institution, not a residual category.

**What would make it the right answer:** Persistent democratic majorities for redistribution that survive political cycles, and an eventual reform of property law that distributes ownership without relying on commons governance.

## A2. Degrowth

**The claim:** The framework's "selective abundance" framing is too generous. The honest answer is degrowth: planned reduction of throughput in wealthy economies, especially energy and material consumption, to fit within planetary boundaries. Commons governance is necessary but secondary; the binding constraint is total throughput.

**Best advocates:** Jason Hickel (*Less is More*), Giorgos Kallis, Kate Raworth (*Doughnut Economics*, partially).

**Strongest evidence:** Decoupling economic growth from material throughput at the rates required has not been observed at any meaningful scale. Wealthy economies already exceed multiple planetary boundaries. Continued growth in those economies is incompatible with 1.5°C climate targets.

**What it gets right:**
- The throughput question is binding and Commonweave does not currently answer it directly.
- "Abundance" framing risks importing growth-economy assumptions into a framework that should resist them.
- Material limits matter and the framework should price them in.

**Where it conflicts with Commonweave:**
- Commonweave's "trending toward abundance" category may be too permissive.
- Commonweave centers governance; degrowth centers throughput.
- Some degrowth thinkers argue commons frameworks are insufficient without explicit consumption caps.

**What would make it the right answer:** If technological efficiency does not produce decoupling within the available climate window, the binding question becomes consumption reduction, and any framework that doesn't lead with that is misallocating attention.

## A3. Techno-optimist abundance

**The claim:** Genuine post-scarcity is achievable through compounding technical progress: cheap energy (solar + storage), AI-assisted production, advanced manufacturing, gene-edited agriculture. Most of Commonweave's governance machinery becomes unnecessary when the underlying scarcity dissolves. Build the technology; the politics follows.

**Best advocates:** Vinod Khosla, Marc Andreessen (*Techno-Optimist Manifesto*), Peter Diamandis (*Abundance*).

**Strongest evidence:** Solar costs have collapsed faster than any economist predicted. AI is automating cognitive labor at unprecedented rates. Synthetic biology is on a similar curve. Many "scarce" goods may not stay scarce.

**What it gets right:**
- Technology curves are real and have lifted billions out of poverty.
- Governance frameworks designed for scarcity can become obsolete fast.
- Pessimism about technical progress has a poor track record.

**Where it conflicts with Commonweave:**
- Commonweave argues the *distribution* of abundance is governed, regardless of the supply curve. Cheap solar plus extractive ownership is not the post-labor economy.
- Persistent scarcities (land, attention, care) are not technology problems and are unlikely to be solved technically.
- Techno-optimist framings often elide the political question entirely.

**What would make it the right answer:** If automation and material abundance arrive faster than ownership inequality compounds, and if governance lag does not produce capture, the technology pathway might genuinely outrun the governance pathway. (Commonweave's bet is that it won't.)

## A4. Platform cooperativism

**The claim:** The post-labor economy is best built by replacing extractive platforms (Uber, Airbnb, gig-work brokers) with worker- and user-owned alternatives running on the same digital infrastructure. The legal-economic vehicle is the platform co-op, not the broader "mycelial network" of commons institutions.

**Best advocates:** Trebor Scholz (*Platform Cooperativism*), Nathan Schneider (*Everything for Everyone*).

**Strongest evidence:** Stocksy, Up & Go, Drivers Cooperative, Eva, and a growing roster of platform co-ops demonstrate that the model is viable. The legal frameworks for cooperatives are mature in most jurisdictions.

**What it gets right:**
- A specific, replicable, legally recognized vehicle.
- Direct counter to the most visible form of extractive labor today.
- Practical, not aspirational.

**Where it conflicts with Commonweave:**
- Platform cooperativism is narrower than Commonweave's framework — it doesn't address land, ecology, care, or governance beyond worker-firm boundaries.
- Critics argue platform co-ops scale poorly compared to VC-backed competitors and risk capture by capital markets.
- Commonweave's "Mycelial Strategy" includes platform co-ops but treats them as one tile, not the picture.

**What would make it the right answer:** If platform co-ops scale faster than other commons forms and become the dominant alternative to capital-backed platforms, the rest of the framework is decorative.

## A5. Municipalism / Bookchin-Öcalan

**The claim:** The right scale for commons governance is the municipality and the confederation of municipalities. National-state frameworks are too captured; global frameworks too thin. Build democratic confederations from the city up. Rojava and Kurdish democratic confederalism are the operational test cases.

**Best advocates:** Murray Bookchin (*The Ecology of Freedom*), Abdullah Öcalan (*Democratic Confederalism*), Debbie Bookchin.

**Strongest evidence:** Rojava has run a multi-million-person confederation under democratic-confederal principles for over a decade under wartime conditions. Spanish Civil War CNT-FAI federations achieved scale before being destroyed externally.

**What it gets right:**
- Specific operational scale.
- Strong empirical case under hostile conditions.
- Centers political form, not just economic ownership.

**Where it conflicts with Commonweave:**
- Commonweave is multi-scalar; municipalism argues the municipal scale is privileged.
- Municipalism is more openly anti-state; Commonweave is more pluralist about state roles.
- Commonweave's directory currently underrepresents municipalist examples.

**What would make it the right answer:** If commons governance fails reliably above the municipal scale, and if confederation succeeds reliably between municipalities, the framework should reorganize around city-scale primary units.

## A6. Labor revival

**The claim:** The actual lever for a more equitable economy is collective bargaining, sectoral bargaining, and labor power. Commons governance is a distraction from the boring, proven mechanism: organize workplaces, raise wages, expand benefits, codify rights. Rebuild what was dismantled between 1980 and 2020.

**Best advocates:** Jane McAlevey (*No Shortcuts*), Kim Moody, Robert Reich.

**Strongest evidence:** The wage share, hours worked, and worker safety in the high-density-union era of the mid-20th century outperformed every period before or since. Sectoral bargaining (Germany, Nordic countries) demonstrably raises wages and reduces inequality. The mechanism is well-understood.

**What it gets right:**
- A concrete, legally-recognized mechanism.
- Strong historical track record.
- Targets the actual point of capital-labor conflict.

**Where it conflicts with Commonweave:**
- Labor revival accepts capital-labor employment as the basic unit. Commonweave argues the post-labor economy must move beyond that.
- Labor power is a within-firm mechanism. Commonweave's framework includes care, ecology, and commons that don't fit within firms.
- Some labor-revival advocates view cooperative ownership as a niche distraction.

**What would make it the right answer:** If labor power can be rebuilt at mid-century density within the available climate and automation window, much of Commonweave becomes redundant.

## A7. Eco-localism / bioregionalism

**The claim:** The right organizing principle is the bioregion: watersheds, foodsheds, ecosystems. Build economies that fit their bioregions rather than scale-up commons institutions across continents. Commonweave's global directory may be the wrong artifact entirely.

**Best advocates:** Wendell Berry, Kirkpatrick Sale, Stephanie Mills.

**Strongest evidence:** Local food systems, watershed-based water governance, and bioregional planning consistently outperform top-down equivalents on resilience and ecological outcomes.

**What it gets right:**
- Centers ecology, not just economics.
- Treats place as primary, not abstract scale.
- Resilience benefits are real.

**Where it conflicts with Commonweave:**
- Commonweave's global directory is implicitly anti-localist.
- Bioregional thinking sometimes reduces to provincialism or "small is beautiful" parochialism.
- Cross-bioregional coordination (climate, pandemics, migration) is hard to fit into pure bioregionalism.

**What would make it the right answer:** If commons institutions reliably outperform when bounded to bioregions and fail when stretched across them, the directory should be a federation of bioregional sub-directories, not a global one.

## A8. Public AI infrastructure

**The claim:** The most consequential commons of the next decade is compute, training data, and AI capability. Whoever owns those owns the post-labor economy regardless of what governance happens elsewhere. Build public-AI infrastructure (public compute, public training corpora, public model weights, democratic AI governance) before everything else.

**Best advocates:** Open-source AI advocates (Yann LeCun, Stella Biderman/EleutherAI), public-option-AI proposals, AI Now Institute reports.

**Strongest evidence:** AI capability is the single fastest-compounding technology of the current era. Closed AI labs are concentrating power at unprecedented rates. Compute is the binding constraint.

**What it gets right:**
- Names the binding technology of the decade.
- Treats AI as commons-vs-enclosure question, not just labor question.
- Specific actionable infrastructure (public compute, open weights, training-data commons).

**Where it conflicts with Commonweave:**
- Commonweave's framework currently underweights AI infrastructure as a category.
- If AI infrastructure becomes the binding commons, the rest of the framework is downstream.

**What would make it the right answer:** If AI capability concentration outruns governance response in the next 3-5 years, public-AI infrastructure becomes a prerequisite for every other commons project. (Arguably already is.)

## A9. Neo-feudal stability (a risk scenario, not an advocacy position)

**The claim:** The most likely outcome is not post-scarcity, post-labor, or commons-governed. It is a stable equilibrium where capital-owners (now augmented by AI) own most production, a small skilled-services class serves them, and the remainder of the population subsists on transfer payments and entertainment. This is not a desirable scenario, but it may be the realistic one.

**Best advocates:** Yanis Varoufakis (*Technofeudalism*), Joel Kotkin (*The Coming of Neo-Feudalism*), some accelerationist-pessimist writing.

**Strongest evidence:** Wealth concentration trends. Platform-mediated economic relationships ("rent" rather than "profit") already approximate this. AI augmentation of capital may stabilize the equilibrium.

**What it gets right:**
- Names a likely failure mode of the current trajectory.
- Doesn't assume good outcomes will arrive on their own.
- Forces the question: what *prevents* this?

**Where it conflicts with Commonweave:**
- Commonweave argues this is the failure mode to prevent, not the realistic outcome.
- If neo-feudalism is the realistic equilibrium, Commonweave is fighting gravity.

**What would make it the relevant analysis:** If wealth concentration continues at current rates and democratic capacity for redistribution does not recover, neo-feudal stability becomes the likely outcome and Commonweave should be honest about competing against it rather than pretending it doesn't exist.

---

## Adding an alternative

When adding a steelman alternative:

1. State the claim as its strongest advocates would state it. No strawmen.
2. Cite the best advocates and the strongest evidence. If you can't, you don't understand it well enough yet.
3. Be honest about what it gets right that Commonweave doesn't.
4. Be specific about where the disagreement actually lives.
5. State what would make the alternative the right answer instead.

If you can't write a steelman of an alternative, you can't dismiss it.

# Open Problem: How Do You Govern a Commons Without Creating a New Elite?

**Category: Governance | Difficulty: Ongoing | Status: Early draft, needs sharpening**

---

This is the problem that has killed more commons projects than funding ever did.

The framework proposes common ownership of land, water, energy, data, and infrastructure. It proposes democratic governance of those commons. It proposes that this governance be leaderless and structureless.

That last part is a problem we've had to confront directly, and we're not done with it.

## Jo Freeman's critique

In 1972, Jo Freeman wrote "The Tyranny of Structurelessness." She was writing about the women's liberation movement, but the argument applies everywhere: *there is no such thing as a structureless group.* When you refuse to formalize leadership, you don't eliminate it. You make it invisible and unaccountable. Cliques form. People with more time, more social capital, and more comfort speaking up exercise outsized influence. And because the leadership isn't acknowledged, it can't be challenged.

Occupy is the textbook modern case. It didn't fail because it had leaders. It failed because it refused to acknowledge the leaders it had, and so couldn't hold them accountable, replace them, or make decisions when it needed to.

The framework says "no personality cult can form around a leaderless structure." That's empirically false. Personality cults form around ideas and aesthetic identities, not just named individuals. Anonymous projects can develop cult-like followings. The absence of a named leader doesn't prevent hero worship - it just removes accountability.

## Elinor Ostrom's counter

The good news is that commons governance has been studied rigorously. Ostrom's Nobel-winning work identified eight design principles that distinguish commons that persist from commons that collapse:

1. Clearly defined boundaries (who's in, who's out)
2. Rules adapted to local conditions
3. Collective choice arrangements (those affected by the rules can modify them)
4. Monitoring
5. Graduated sanctions
6. Conflict resolution mechanisms
7. Recognition of rights to organize (external authorities don't undermine local governance)
8. For larger systems: nested enterprises (governance at multiple scales)

Notice what's on that list: monitoring, sanctions, and conflict resolution. Not just cooperation - accountability. Ostrom's commons work is often cited to mean "commons work." It actually means "commons work *when these specific conditions are met*, and collapse when they aren't."

## The question we're wrestling with

The framework wants commons governance that is:
- Genuinely democratic (majority can't override minority rights or ecological limits - but who enforces that?)
- Locally adapted (community decides its own rules - but what stops local majorities from excluding outsiders?)
- Non-hierarchical (no permanent class of administrators - but someone has to manage the water table)
- Transparent (all decisions visible - but visibility alone doesn't prevent capture by those with more time to show up)

These are in tension. We haven't resolved the tensions. Some specific questions:

**The unglamorous work problem.** If contribution is voluntary and people do what they find meaningful, who runs the sewage plant at 3am? The kibbutz movement tried voluntarism and eventually had to implement work quotas and task rotation. Mondragón has management. Most intentional communities have assigned roles. What's the Ecolibrium answer?

**The scale problem.** Ostrom's design principles work for bounded, identifiable communities - fishing villages, irrigation systems, alpine meadows. It's unclear how they scale to a city's water system, let alone global data infrastructure. What do we actually know about scaling commons governance?

**The democratic override problem.** What happens when a democratic majority votes to allow resource extraction that violates planetary boundaries? The framework says ecological limits are constitutional - they override democratic decisions. But who enforces that? A court? An unelected scientific body? Either answer re-introduces hierarchy.

**The transition problem.** Even if the end-state governance is solid, the transition governance is a different problem. During transition, someone is deciding what gets built, what gets merged into the commons first, what the rules are while the rules are being written. That's enormous power. Who holds it?

## What we're asking for

- Is there a contemporary commons governance model (at meaningful scale - not a single village) that handles the unglamorous work problem without either coercion or permanent underclass?
- What does Ostrom's work actually say about scaling? Her later work on "governing the knowledge commons" with Charlotte Hess is directly relevant - has anyone applied it to digital infrastructure specifically?
- The Wikimedia Foundation model (community governance, centralized infrastructure) and the Apache Foundation model (umbrella organization, federated projects) both work in practice. Are these compromises worth studying seriously, or do they reproduce the problems we're trying to avoid?
- What do we get wrong when we cite Ostrom? Where does our application of her work overreach?

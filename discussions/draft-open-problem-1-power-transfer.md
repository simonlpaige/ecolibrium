# Open Problem: Why Would Anyone With Power Give It Up?

**Category: Theory of Change | Difficulty: Hard | Status: Unsolved**

---

This is the question we've been least honest about, and we're posting it here because we'd rather be embarrassed by the gap than pretend it doesn't exist.

The framework describes a three-phase transition from the current economic arrangement to one based on common ownership, voluntary contribution, and ecological equilibrium. Phase 2 is "the transfer." The old document called it the moment when the old system "empties out" as people voluntarily migrate to better alternatives.

That's not a theory. That's a wish.

## The actual problem

Power does not become irrelevant just because better alternatives exist. Renewable energy has been cheaper than new coal-fired generation in most markets since roughly 2019. Coal still receives roughly $7 trillion in annual global subsidies. The fossil fuel industry didn't gracefully accept obsolescence - it bought regulatory protection against it.

Same story: taxi medallions vs. Uber. Incumbent telecoms vs. municipal broadband. Big Pharma vs. generics. The pattern is consistent. Entrenched power adapts, captures regulation, and slows its own obsolescence by decades. "Eventually" is real, but it can mean two generations of harm in the meantime.

Our [CRITIQUE.md](../CRITIQUE.md) puts it bluntly: *"A billionaire is not going to voluntarily become a regular participant in a commons because you offered them dignity. Their entire identity, social network, and material reality is organized around not being equal."*

We think this is right. So what do we do with it?

## What the research says

Chenoweth and Stephan's dataset (323 campaigns, 1900-2006) shows nonviolent campaigns succeed roughly twice as often as violent ones - 53% vs. 26%. The mechanism is participation scale: nonviolent movements can recruit more broadly because the physical risk is lower.

Gene Sharp catalogued 198 methods of nonviolent action, and the ones that actually transferred power did so by withdrawing cooperation from systems that depended on it - not by waiting for those systems to become "irrelevant."

The post-Arab Spring data is sobering though. Chenoweth herself has written about a recent decline in nonviolent resistance effectiveness - improved autocrat coordination, digital surveillance, erosion of independent civil society. The historical dataset may not hold forward.

## Where we're stuck

We can identify several mechanisms that have historically moved power:

1. **Economic obsolescence** - works when switching costs are low and incumbents can't capture regulation. Rare.
2. **Regulatory capture in reverse** - electing people committed to commons-based policy. Works slowly, vulnerable to backsliding.
3. **Coalition pressure** - labor, boycotts, shareholder activism. Works at the margins.
4. **Withdrawing cooperation** - Sharp's model. Requires scale and willingness to bear costs.
5. **A crisis creating a window** - historically the most reliable, also the most dangerous (see: Naomi Klein's shock doctrine problem - crises open windows for power grabs in both directions).

What we don't have: a theory of why any of these is sufficient for the scale of transfer Ecolibrium describes, rather than incremental reform within existing systems.

## What we're asking for

- Has anyone modeled a plausible full-scale commons transition that accounts for power resistance, not just governance design?
- Is the right framing "power transfer" at all, or is it something else - erosion, encirclement, substitution?
- The Chile 1973 case haunts everything in this document. Allende's government was doing many things right by democratic legitimacy standards. It didn't matter. What's the honest response to that history?
- Is there a version of this framework that works under conditions of *partial* transfer - where some sectors transition and others don't, for decades?

We're not looking for reassurance. We're looking for sharper thinking than we currently have.

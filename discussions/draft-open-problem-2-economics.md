# Open Problem: The Economics Don't Add Up Yet

**Category: Economics | Difficulty: Hard | Status: Placeholder, not a plan**

---

We're going to be direct: the economics section of this framework is currently a wish list, not a model. This post exists to name that honestly and invite people who actually know this stuff to help fix it.

## What we have

The framework proposes: universal basic income, wealth caps, cooperative ownership conversion, debt jubilee, dissolution of monopolies, dismantling of tax havens.

Each of these is a *goal*. None of them currently has, in our documents:

- An order-of-magnitude cost estimate
- A proposed funding mechanism
- An honest account of transition costs and who bears them
- A model for what happens when these policies are announced (capital flight, political backlash, second-order effects)

## The specific gaps we know about

**UBI at scale.** The OpenResearch study (Stockton, CA - $1,000/month, 1,000 people, 3 years) showed lifestyle improvements. We cite it as evidence UBI works. But the study's own findings were mixed on labor market effects - participants worked slightly fewer hours and earned slightly less income. And scaling to the US population alone would cost roughly $4 trillion per year - about the entire current federal budget. We don't have a funding theory for that.

The Marshall Islands' $200/quarter UBI is a meaningful transfer for 42,000 people funded by a finite trust. It is not a model for 8 billion people, and we've been sloppy about implying it's directionally similar.

**Wealth caps and capital flight.** If a country announces a hard wealth cap, capital leaves before the cap takes effect. This is not a theoretical risk - it's what happens. The framework doesn't address: what are the countermeasures? Coordinated international implementation? Capital controls? We don't know, and we should say so.

**Debt jubilee and pension funds.** A lot of sovereign and consumer debt is held by pension funds and retirement savings vehicles. A debt jubilee that cancels that debt transfers the loss to retirees. The framework doesn't name this tradeoff. It should.

**Cooperative conversion.** Mondragon is our main cited precedent. It's a real, valuable model. It's also had significant struggles - the Fagor Electrodomésticos bankruptcy in 2013 wiped out thousands of worker-owners' savings. It's had to rely increasingly on non-member temporary workers who don't receive cooperative benefits. Italy's Marcora Law offers a different model - workers can use their unemployment benefits as seed capital to convert failing firms to co-ops. That's interesting and undersold in our research. What else exists?

## What we're not looking for

We're not looking for "here's why your goals are wrong." We know there are strong arguments that wealth caps are counterproductive, that UBI discourages work, that cooperative firms underinvest in capital. We've read those arguments. We're interested in the harder question: **if the goals are approximately right, what are the most credible mechanisms for achieving them at scale?**

## What we're asking for

- Is there a serious economic model (not a blog post, an actual model) for funding UBI at national scale through land value tax, automation dividend, or sovereign wealth? We know about the Alaska Permanent Fund. What else has been stress-tested?
- Mondragon and the Emilia-Romagna cooperative economy are our main scale evidence. What are we missing? What's the strongest counter-evidence from cooperative economics?
- The framework currently treats "selective abundance" (some goods trending toward zero marginal cost, others persistently scarce) as its economic foundation. Is that framing coherent, or does it break down somewhere we haven't seen?
- Who's doing the most rigorous work on cooperative transition financing right now? Not advocacy - research.

We'd rather have a shorter document that's honest about what it doesn't know than a long one that implies confidence we don't have.

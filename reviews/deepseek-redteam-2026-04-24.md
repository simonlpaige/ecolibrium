## 1. Framing and Paradigm Blind Spots

### The Core Problem Definition Is Liberal-Individualist and OECD-Centric
The guide opens with "surplus hours on one side, a roof shortage on the other." This frames the issue as a market-clearing problem - matching idle labor to unmet need. But housing is not primarily a production problem in most of the world; it's a distribution and financialization problem. There are more empty homes than unhoused people in many crisis markets. Framing the solution as *building more homes* accepts a supply-side diagnosis that is contested, especially by tenant unions and housing-justice movements who argue the primary fix is decommodification of *existing* stock, vacancy taxes, and rent control - not sweat-equity construction on the periphery.

**Counter-argument to address:** "You're solving a construction pipeline problem when the actual crisis is speculative land hoarding and financialized rental extraction. Why build new CLT homes on expensive land when seizing or decommodifying existing vacant units would be cheaper, faster, and less environmentally damaging?"

### "Surplus Hours" as an Unproblematic Category
The guide treats "people running out of paid work" as a pool of ready labor. This elides:
- **The deskilling problem.** A displaced call-center worker or junior copywriter cannot be retooled into a framer in six months. The guide mentions trades apprenticeships but underestimates the time and physical-capacity requirements.
- **The geography mismatch.** Surplus workers are concentrated in cities; buildable land (and CLTs) are often peripheral or rural.
- **The care-work blind spot.** Who is watching the children while the displaced worker does 400 hours of sweat equity? The guide mentions childcare as an "alternate pathway" for disability access but doesn't treat it as a structural requirement for *any* parent, especially single parents, who are disproportionately low-income.

**Missing section:** A frank appraisal of *who this model cannot serve* - not just people with disabilities, but single parents of young children, the elderly, the chronically ill, people in precarious gig work with unpredictable schedules.

### The Metaphor of "Pipeline" Is Industrial
Calling it a "democratic pipeline" frames humans as inputs to be processed. The guide is self-aware about company towns, but the language itself ("pipeline," "stitching," "pipeline-external work") treats people as transferable resource units. This may alienate the very community organizers the guide hopes to reach, who prefer ecological or relational metaphors.

## 2. Western/American Legal and Structural Assumptions

### The Guide Is Functionally a US Document with a Few European Wave-Ins
Despite naming international examples, the legal scaffolding (Part 4) is overwhelmingly US: 501(c)(3), Davis-Bacon, LIHTC, HUD SHOP, AMI, Fair Housing Act. A reader in Kenya, India, or Brazil will find almost nothing actionable. The "Outside the US, the wrappers are different" paragraph gestures at this but doesn't do the work. The CLT model in the Global South often involves customary land tenure, communal titles, or state-owned land leased to cooperatives - none of which are addressed.

**Recommendation:** Either retitle the guide "A US-Centric Field Guide with Some International Notes" or add substantial appendices on non-US legal wrappers (the Kenyan SACCO-to-housing pipeline, the Uruguayan cooperativas de vivienda, the Philippine community mortgage program, the Indian condominium act's cooperative housing provisions).

### The Union Leg Assumes Northern/Western Industrial Unionism
The guide's labor model is the Northern trades union with an apprenticeship program, a joint trust fund, and a PLA. This doesn't translate to:
- **Majority-informal economies** where construction labor is casual, unorganized, and often migrant.
- **Political unionism traditions** (South Africa, Brazil, Philippines) where unions are social movements first and collective-bargaining entities second, and "signing a PLA with a CLT" may not be the right institutional form.
- **State-corporatist union structures** (China, Vietnam, some Gulf states) where the union is an arm of the state or the ruling party.
- **Gig-economy organizing** where construction workers may be organized through app-based platforms or worker centers, not traditional locals.

### AMI and 80% AMI Are US-Specific, and Problematic Even There
The guide uses 80% AMI as the default affordability target. This is a US HUD metric. But even in the US, "80% AMI" in high-cost coastal cities is a middle-class income. In San Francisco, 80% AMI for a family of four is over $130,000. The guide acknowledges this variance but doesn't interrogate the political problem: a pipeline that serves households at 80% AMI may be building housing for the relatively privileged, not the most housing-insecure. Community organizers will ask, "Who is this actually for?" The guide needs a clear stance on *which* income tier it's targeting and why.

## 3. Political Economy and Power Analysis Gaps

### The Guide Is Apolitical About Urban Governance
Step 1 for "if you are neither" is "Find the land." This is where the actual political war is. Surplus public land is contested by for-profit developers, NIMBY homeowners' associations, and intra-governmental turf battles (school district vs. city vs. transit agency). The guide offers no strategy for winning that fight - no coalition-building with tenant unions, no electoral strategy, no land-banking through adverse possession or community land purchase campaigns. It assumes a rational actor city government that will happily ground-lease land to a CLT. This is fantasy in most US cities.

### The Developer and Finance Industry Are Absent as Antagonists
The guide names no enemies. Who benefits from the current housing regime? Private developers, institutional landlords (Blackstone, etc.), single-family rental REITs, mortgage-finance incumbents, and the zoning-and-permitting apparatus that serves them. A field guide that doesn't name the forces arrayed against its model is not preparing its readers for the fight. The guide critiques Habitat's gentrification effects gently; it never critiques, say, the California Association of Realtors' role in killing social housing legislation.

### The "Subsidy Gap" Discussion Normalizes Public Austerity
The guide says a pilot "cannot be self-funding" and requires subsidy. It then lists funding sources. But it doesn't ask the obvious political question: if public subsidy is required, and the public is already paying billions in mortgage-interest deductions, LIHTC, and developer tax breaks, why is the subsidy for a democratic, permanently-affordable model "a gap that has to be funded" while the subsidy for market-rate development is infrastructure? This is a framing problem. The guide should argue that redirecting existing housing subsidies (which are regressive) toward CLT pipelines is not "filling a gap" but correcting a market distortion.

## 4. Missing Critical Perspectives

### Gender Analysis Is Almost Entirely Absent
The guide mentions women in the context of union exclusion (briefly). It does not discuss:
- The gendered division of construction labor.
- The fact that sweat-equity models historically assume a male breadwinner doing construction and a female partner doing childcare/administrative hours. The alternate-hours pathway is framed as an exception for disability, not as a structural recognition of care work.
- The vulnerability of women in mixed-gender construction environments, especially when the "supervisor" is a male journeyman and the "volunteer" is a woman earning her home.

### Environmental and Climate Considerations
Building new single-family homes (the assumed typology in the budget: 1,200 sq ft, stick-built, on land with new utilities and roads) is carbon-intensive and land-consumptive. The guide doesn't address:
- Adaptive reuse of existing buildings.
- Dense, multifamily CLT models (mid-rise co-ops on CLT land).
- Embodied carbon, material choices, or climate-resilient siting.
- The tension between "permanently affordable homeownership" and climate retreat from floodplains and fire zones.

### Indigenous Land Back
In settler-colonial states (US, Canada, Australia, New Zealand), any "community land trust" sits on unceded Indigenous land. The guide doesn't acknowledge this. A CLT that doesn't have a memorandum of understanding or land-rematriation commitment with local Indigenous nations is perpetuating a colonial property regime, however progressive its internal governance. This is a live critique of the CLT movement from Indigenous organizers.

## 5. Internal Contradictions and Weaknesses

### The "Firewall" Is Weaker Than Advertised
The firewall (Part 7) says democratic ownership of land, resident control, and hard resale restrictions prevent company-town dynamics. But:
- A CLT board that is one-third residents is not resident-controlled; it's resident-influenced. The other two-thirds (community members and technical experts) can outvote residents. In a hostile takeover scenario, this is a real vulnerability.
- Resale restrictions that keep the home "affordable" also cap wealth-building for the very families the pipeline claims to serve. The guide acknowledges this as a "design problem" but doesn't resolve it. A CLT homeowner in a gentrifying neighborhood watches market-rate neighbors build six-figure equity; she gets a 2% annual bump. The guide calls this "permanent affordability"; a critic calls it "permanent wealth exclusion."

### "Democratic Pipeline" Doesn't Specify Democracy Where
The guide emphasizes democratic governance in the union (one member, one vote) and the co-op (one worker, one vote) and the CLT (tripartite board). It does not specify how decisions get made *across* these entities. If the union, the CLT, and the homeowners disagree about the resale formula, who prevails? What is the governance of the pipeline *itself*? The document gestures at a "federation of CLTs and unions" in Part 8 but this is the hardest institutional-design problem, and it's deferred to an unspecified future.

### Overreliance on "Lawyer Up" as a Solution
Every hard legal question ends with "get project counsel," "get a housing-nonprofit CPA," "plan to spend $25k-$75k on legal review." This is honest, but it also means the model is only accessible to well-resourced organizations. A group of displaced workers in a deindustrialized town with no existing CLT, no friendly union local, and no philanthropic connections cannot use this guide. The guide implicitly admits this ("If you are neither... find the CLT, find the union") but presents it as a sequence, not as a class barrier to entry.

## 6. Substantive Factual and Tone Concerns

### The ITUC/ACTU/COTU Listing in Part 2 Reads as Padding
Naming the ITUC, ACTU, COTU, SEWA, etc. as examples of "the labor leg" is misleading. SEWA is an organization of informal women workers in India; it is not a construction-trades union and would not be a party to a PLA. Including them makes the directory look globally comprehensive but functionally, these organizations are not participants in the pipeline model described. This will irritate informed labor readers.

### "Commonweave" Self-References
Part 8's description of Commonweave as "what Commonweave is/isn't" reads as promotional for a "directory of about 27,000 entries, of which 1,700 are tagged as formal legal entities." That's a lot of unverified data. A red-team reviewer would ask: "How many of those 27,000 are duplicates, defunct, or misclassified? What's the quality-control process? Publishing a CC0 dump of unvetted Wikidata imports is not the same as a curated directory." The guide should be more honest about the directory's limitations.

### Tone of Quiet Certitude
The guide's voice is confident and epigrammatic. This is effective rhetoric, but a critic will mine it for overstatement. The guide would be stronger if it flagged more unknowns - especially the tax treatment of sweat-equity credit, which it admits has no dispositive IRS ruling. The removal of the incorrect revenue ruling citation in the current draft is good; the guide should go further and say, "We are operating in a legal gray zone here, and organizers should understand that a hostile administration could change the interpretation."

## Summary of Key Gaps to Address

1. **Add a "Who This Model Cannot Serve" section** - single parents, the very poor, the deskilled, the geographically mismatched.
2. **Add a political strategy appendix** - how to win land, funding, and zoning changes against organized opposition.
3. **Add a non-US legal appendix** that actually does the work, or retitle as US-focused.
4. **Add a gender and care-work analysis**, not just a disability-access paragraph.
5. **Add an Indigenous land acknowledgment and rematriation framework** for settler-colonial contexts.
6. **Add a climate and density section** - single-family stick-built on greenfield is not the only or best typology.
7. **Be honest about the class access problem** - this model requires institutional capital and expertise that displaced workers don't have without a patron.
8. **Resolve or flag the resident-control vs. tripartite-board tension** in the firewall.
9. **Quality-control disclosure** for the 27,000-entry directory.
10. **Name the opposition.** A field guide that doesn't describe the predators in the ecosystem is incomplete.
